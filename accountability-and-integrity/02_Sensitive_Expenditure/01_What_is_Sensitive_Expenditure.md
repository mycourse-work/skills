# What is Sensitive Expenditure?

Public servants have a responsibility to spend public money wisely and with care. Sensitive expenditure refers to spending that requires extra scrutiny because it could attract public criticism or embarrassment if not handled appropriately.

## Learning Objectives

- Define sensitive expenditure and explain why it requires careful management
- Identify the categories of expenditure that are typically considered sensitive
- Understand the "reasonable person" test as applied to expenditure decisions
- Know your obligations when incurring or approving sensitive expenditure

## Definition

Sensitive expenditure is spending that, if it became publicly known, could reasonably embarrass the organisation or the government. It includes expenditure that might look wasteful, extravagant, or inappropriate — even if it was technically within the rules.

The test is not just whether spending was technically permitted. The question is: **would a reasonable member of the public, knowing the full circumstances, consider this to be a good use of public money?**

> **Important:** The Controller and Auditor-General (OAG) provides guidance on sensitive expenditure for all New Zealand public sector organisations. This guidance sets the standard of behaviour expected of public servants.

## Why Sensitive Expenditure Matters

Public money is a public trust. When public servants spend money on behalf of the government, they are spending money that belongs to New Zealand taxpayers. Accordingly, spending decisions are held to a high standard of accountability.

Poor expenditure decisions can:

- Damage trust in the public service and the government
- Attract negative media coverage and public criticism
- Result in formal audits, investigations, or adverse findings from the Controller and Auditor-General
- Harm careers and organisational reputations

Even relatively small amounts of money can become significant issues if the spending appears inappropriate.

## The Reasonable Person Test

When deciding whether expenditure is appropriate, ask yourself:

*"If a journalist, an auditor, or a member of the public were to see this expense, would they consider it a good use of public money?"*

If the answer is no — or even "maybe not" — you should think very carefully before incurring the expense. Consider whether there is a more cost-effective alternative, whether the expense is truly necessary, and whether it serves a legitimate business purpose.

> **Tip:** If in doubt, don't. It is always better to seek approval before incurring an expense than to explain it afterwards.

## Common Categories of Sensitive Expenditure

The following types of expenditure are generally considered sensitive and require careful management:

- **Hospitality** — meals, functions, drinks, and entertainment provided or attended using public funds
- **Travel** — domestic and international travel, including class of travel, accommodation standards, and personal stopovers
- **Gifts and koha** — gifts given or received in connection with public duties
- **Conferences and events** — attendance and sponsorship costs
- **Credit card use** — purchases made on government credit cards
- **Sensitive personal benefits** — clothing, grooming, vehicles used for personal purposes

Each of these categories is covered in detail in the following lessons.

## The Public Sector Standard

The New Zealand public sector applies a higher standard than private sector organisations. This reflects the fact that public servants are stewards of public money and are accountable to the public, not just to their employer.

This means that spending which might be entirely reasonable in a private company context may not be appropriate in the public sector. For example, a business-class flight might be justified in a commercial context but requires specific justification when paid for by the taxpayer.

## Key Takeaways

- Sensitive expenditure is spending that could attract public criticism or embarrassment
- The test is whether a reasonable person would consider it a good use of public money
- Public money is a public trust — the standard is higher than in the private sector
- When in doubt, seek approval before spending, not after
