# Rules and Expectations for Sensitive Expenditure

The New Zealand public sector has clear rules and expectations about how public money should be spent. These rules apply to all public servants, from frontline staff to chief executives.

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the key rules governing sensitive expenditure in the NZ public service
- Know what approvals are required before incurring sensitive expenditure
- Understand the importance of documentation and record-keeping
- Recognise the consequences of breaching sensitive expenditure rules

## The Overarching Principle

The starting point for all expenditure decisions is the **public interest test**: spending must serve a genuine business purpose and provide value for money. Public servants must be able to justify every expense they incur or approve.

The Controller and Auditor-General's guidance on sensitive expenditure emphasises that spending should be:

- **Necessary** — there is a genuine business need for the expense
- **Proportionate** — the cost is reasonable relative to the benefit
- **Transparent** — records are kept and spending can be accounted for
- **Appropriate** — the type and level of spending is suitable for the public sector context

## Authorisation Requirements

Most sensitive expenditure requires prior authorisation. This means getting approval **before** the expense is incurred, not after. Key rules include:

**Hospitality:** Hospitality above threshold amounts generally requires manager approval. The threshold varies by organisation, but even low-value hospitality should be documented.

**Travel:** Travel bookings must comply with agency travel policies. International travel typically requires higher-level approval. Use of business class requires specific justification.

**Gifts:** Giving or receiving gifts in connection with official duties must comply with agency gifts policies. This includes koha given to tangata whenua as part of official engagements.

**Credit cards:** Government credit cards must only be used for approved business purposes. Personal use — even with the intention of repaying — is not permitted.

> **Warning:** Retroactive approval (seeking approval after the fact) is not the same as proper authorisation. If you incur expenditure without approval, you may still be personally liable even if approval is later granted.

## Documentation Requirements

All sensitive expenditure must be properly documented. Documentation serves multiple purposes:

1. It provides the audit trail required for accountability
2. It protects you if your spending is questioned
3. It enables your organisation to manage its budget effectively
4. It demonstrates that the spending was appropriate and authorised

At minimum, documentation should include the amount spent, the date, the business purpose, the names of any people involved, and the authorising officer.

> **Tip:** Keep receipts for all business expenses. Many agencies require receipts for any expense above a minimal threshold (often as low as $20-$50).

## Scenarios from the Sensitive Expenditure Module

### Scenario: The Team Lunch

A manager decides to take her team of six people out for lunch to celebrate completing a major project. The total cost is $280.

**Key considerations:**
- Is there a genuine business purpose? (Team recognition can be legitimate)
- Is the cost proportionate? ($280 for six people is modest)
- Has the manager obtained prior approval?
- Is this within policy limits for her agency?

**What should happen:** The manager should check her agency's hospitality policy for approval requirements before booking. She should keep the receipt and document the business purpose.

### Scenario: The Conference Upgrade

A public servant is booked on an economy flight to a conference. At the airport, he upgrades to business class using his government credit card, spending an extra $450 of his own accord.

**The problem:** The upgrade was not authorised. Using a government credit card for an unauthorised upgrade is a misuse of public funds, even if economy class was already approved.

**What should happen:** He should have sought approval for the upgrade in advance, or paid for it personally. After the fact, he must report the unauthorised use and repay or account for the difference.

## Consequences of Breaching the Rules

Breaching sensitive expenditure rules can have serious consequences:

- **Repayment** — you may be required to repay misspent funds from your own pocket
- **Formal disciplinary action** — up to and including dismissal
- **Adverse findings** — the Controller and Auditor-General may issue a public report criticising the expenditure
- **Reputational damage** — both personal and organisational

Even unintentional breaches can result in disciplinary action if the expenditure was reckless or showed poor judgment.

## Key Takeaways

- All sensitive expenditure must be necessary, proportionate, transparent, and appropriate
- Get approval before spending — retroactive approval is not sufficient
- Keep documentation for all expenses
- Consequences for breaching the rules include repayment, disciplinary action, and reputational damage
