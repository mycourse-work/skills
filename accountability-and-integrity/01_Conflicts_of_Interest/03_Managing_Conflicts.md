# Managing Conflicts of Interest

Once you have identified a conflict of interest, the next step is managing it appropriately. Good management means taking action that protects the public interest, maintains trust, and is transparent.

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the steps involved in managing a conflict of interest
- Know what disclosure means and how to do it
- Understand the range of management options available
- Apply management approaches to realistic scenarios

## The Three-Step Process

Managing a conflict of interest involves three steps: identify, disclose, and manage.

### Step 1: Identify

Recognise that a conflict exists or may exist. Use the "reasonable person" test — if a reasonable, impartial observer might think your interests could influence your decisions, treat it as a conflict.

### Step 2: Disclose

Tell your manager (or the appropriate person in your organisation) about the conflict. Do this as soon as you become aware of it — do not wait until after the decision is made.

Disclosure should be:
- Timely — as soon as you become aware of the conflict
- Clear — describe the nature of the conflict and why it exists
- Documented — keep a record of what was disclosed and when

> **Important:** Many agencies have a conflicts of interest register. Ask your manager or HR team whether your organisation has one and how to use it.

### Step 3: Manage

Work with your manager to agree on how the conflict will be managed. The appropriate management approach will depend on the nature and severity of the conflict.

## Management Options

There is a spectrum of management options, ranging from minor adjustments to complete removal from a process.

**Acknowledge and monitor** — For low-level or perceived conflicts, it may be sufficient to acknowledge the conflict, document it, and continue — with heightened transparency about decision-making.

**Restrict involvement** — You may be able to remain involved in a process but limit your role to reduce the risk of bias. For example, contributing to analysis but not making the final decision.

**Recuse yourself** — Step back from a particular decision or process entirely. A colleague takes over the relevant part of your work.

**Transfer responsibility** — The conflict is ongoing, so responsibility for that area of work is transferred to someone else on a more permanent basis.

**Divest the interest** — In serious cases, you may need to divest yourself of a financial interest (such as selling shares) to remove the conflict entirely.

**Resign from a role** — In extreme cases, it may be necessary to resign from an external role (such as a board membership) that is creating an irreconcilable conflict.

> **Tip:** The right management option is not always the most extreme one. Work with your manager to find a proportionate response that manages the conflict without unnecessarily disrupting your work.

## More Scenarios from Integrity Town

### Scenario 5: Sandra — The Funding Decision

**Situation:** Sandra is a grants manager. Her sister runs a community organisation that has applied for a grant from her agency.

**The conflict:** Sandra has a direct family relationship with an applicant. This is an actual conflict of interest.

**Management:** Sandra must not be involved in assessing or approving her sister's application. She should disclose the conflict immediately and transfer that application to a colleague. She should also ensure she has no access to information about the outcome until after it is finalised.

### Scenario 6: Rawiri — The Reference Check

**Situation:** Rawiri is asked to conduct a reference check on a job applicant. He knows the applicant personally and considers them a friend.

**The conflict:** Rawiri's personal relationship may make it difficult for him to provide an objective reference check.

**Management:** Rawiri should disclose the relationship to the hiring manager and ask for the reference check to be conducted by someone else.

### Scenario 7: James — The Investment

**Situation:** James works in a team that regulates a particular industry. He discovers that he owns shares in a company that his team will soon be making a regulatory decision about.

**The conflict:** James has a direct financial interest in the outcome of a regulatory decision.

**Management:** James should disclose the shareholding immediately. He should recuse himself from any work related to that company's regulatory matter. He should also discuss with his manager whether he needs to divest the shares given his ongoing regulatory role.

### Scenario 8: Hana — The Tender Panel

**Situation:** Hana is on a tender panel evaluating bids from external suppliers. She previously worked for one of the bidding companies.

**The conflict:** Hana has a professional relationship with one of the suppliers that could be perceived as influencing her assessment.

**Management:** Hana should disclose her previous employment to the panel chair. The panel chair will then decide whether Hana should be excluded from evaluating that particular bid, or whether she can continue with appropriate monitoring.

## Documentation is Essential

Whatever management approach is agreed, make sure it is documented. A written record protects you if questions are raised later and demonstrates that the conflict was managed with integrity.

Your documentation should record:
- The nature of the conflict
- When it was disclosed and to whom
- The management approach agreed
- Any conditions placed on your continued involvement

## Key Takeaways

- The three steps are: identify, disclose, and manage
- Disclose as early as possible — do not wait
- Management options range from monitoring to full removal from a process
- Choose a proportionate response, not necessarily the most extreme
- Document everything — it protects you and demonstrates integrity
