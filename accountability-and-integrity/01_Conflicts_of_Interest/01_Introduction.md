# Introduction to Conflicts of Interest

Conflicts of interest are a common challenge in the New Zealand public service. Understanding what they are, how to identify them, and how to manage them is essential for maintaining public trust and upholding the integrity of the public service.

## Learning Objectives

- Understand what a conflict of interest is
- Recognise the difference between actual, potential, and perceived conflicts of interest
- Know your obligations as a public servant when a conflict of interest arises
- Understand the importance of disclosure and transparent management

## What is a Conflict of Interest?

A conflict of interest arises when your personal interests — or the interests of someone close to you — could improperly influence how you carry out your public duties. This can happen even if there is no actual wrongdoing involved.

There are three types of conflicts of interest to be aware of:

**Actual conflict of interest** — A conflict that currently exists where your personal interests directly conflict with your public duties.

**Potential conflict of interest** — A conflict that could arise in the future, or where you can foresee a situation where your interests might conflict with your duties.

**Perceived conflict of interest** — A situation where a reasonable person might think your personal interests could improperly influence your decisions, even if you believe there is no actual conflict.

> **Important:** All three types of conflicts must be managed. Even a perceived conflict can damage public trust and your organisation's reputation.

## Why Conflicts of Interest Matter

Public servants are entrusted with significant responsibilities and public resources. When conflicts of interest go unmanaged, they can:

- Compromise the integrity of decisions made on behalf of the public
- Damage trust in the public service
- Expose you and your organisation to legal and reputational risk
- Lead to unfair outcomes for people affected by those decisions

The New Zealand public service is built on values of integrity, impartiality, and trust. Managing conflicts of interest is fundamental to upholding these values.

## Your Obligations

As a public servant, you have a duty to:

1. **Identify** when a conflict of interest exists or may exist
2. **Disclose** the conflict to your manager or a designated person in your organisation
3. **Manage** the conflict appropriately — this may mean stepping back from a decision, recusing yourself from a process, or divesting yourself of a financial interest

The key principle is transparency. You should not try to manage a conflict of interest alone or in secret. Disclosure protects both you and the public interest.

## The Integrity Town Scenarios

In this module, you will work through a series of scenarios set in a fictional public service organisation. Each scenario presents a real-world situation involving potential conflicts of interest.

As you work through the scenarios, you will be asked to identify what type of conflict exists, what the public servant should do, and why. This will help you build the practical judgment needed to identify and manage conflicts of interest in your own work.

## Key Takeaways

- A conflict of interest can be actual, potential, or perceived — all three types require attention
- You have a duty to disclose conflicts of interest, not to manage them alone
- Transparency is the foundation of good conflict of interest management
- Unmanaged conflicts can damage public trust and expose you to risk
