# Identifying Conflicts of Interest

Identifying a conflict of interest is not always straightforward. Sometimes a conflict is obvious, but often it requires careful thought about your relationships, interests, and the decisions you are involved in.

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the common types of personal interests that can create conflicts
- Apply the "reasonable person" test to identify perceived conflicts
- Recognise conflict of interest situations in realistic public service scenarios
- Know what steps to take when you identify a potential conflict

## Types of Personal Interests

Personal interests that can create conflicts of interest include both financial and non-financial interests.

**Financial interests** include things like shareholdings, investments, business ownership, secondary employment, loans, and financial relationships with organisations that your agency deals with.

**Non-financial interests** include personal relationships (such as family members or close friends), memberships of clubs or organisations, previous employment or professional relationships, political affiliations, and strong personal beliefs or values that may affect your impartiality.

> **Tip:** It is not just your own interests that matter. The interests of people close to you — such as a spouse, partner, family member, or close friend — can also create a conflict of interest.

## The Integrity Town Scenarios

The following scenarios from the Integrity Town eLearning module illustrate common types of conflicts of interest in the public service. Each scenario is based on a real pattern of conflict seen in New Zealand government organisations.

### Scenario 1: Tama — The Job Application

**Situation:** Tama is on a panel interviewing candidates for a position in his team. One of the applicants is his cousin.

**The conflict:** Tama has a personal relationship with one of the candidates. Even if he believes he can be fair, a reasonable person would question whether the relationship influenced the outcome.

**Type of conflict:** Perceived conflict of interest (and potentially actual).

**What should Tama do?** Tama should disclose his relationship with the candidate to his manager before the interview process begins. He should step back from the panel for that candidate's interview, or recuse himself entirely from the selection process if required.

### Scenario 2: Lisa — The Supplier

**Situation:** Lisa is responsible for procuring services from external suppliers. Her brother-in-law runs one of the companies on the approved supplier list.

**The conflict:** Lisa has a financial interest (indirect, through family) in one of the suppliers she works with. This is both a potential and actual conflict.

**Type of conflict:** Actual conflict of interest.

**What should Lisa do?** Lisa should disclose the relationship to her manager. She should remove herself from any procurement decisions involving her brother-in-law's company. A colleague should handle those procurement decisions instead.

### Scenario 3: Mark — The Side Business

**Situation:** Mark works as a policy analyst. He has recently started a small consultancy business on the side, providing advice in the same policy area he works in at his agency.

**The conflict:** Mark's private work could conflict with his public duties. He may have access to confidential information through his government role that could benefit his private clients.

**Type of conflict:** Actual conflict of interest.

**What should Mark do?** Mark must disclose his secondary employment to his employer. Depending on agency policy, he may need approval before continuing the consultancy. He must ensure he does not use government information or resources for private gain.

### Scenario 4: Ruth — The Community Board

**Situation:** Ruth sits on a local community board in a voluntary capacity. Her government agency is now consulting with that community board about a funding decision.

**The conflict:** Ruth is on both sides of a decision her agency is making. Her loyalty to the community board may conflict with her duty to provide impartial advice to her agency.

**Type of conflict:** Actual and perceived conflict of interest.

**What should Ruth do?** Ruth should disclose her board membership to her manager immediately. She should step back from any work related to the funding decision that involves the community board she sits on.

## The "Reasonable Person" Test

A useful tool for identifying conflicts of interest is to ask: "Would a reasonable, impartial person looking at this situation think my personal interests could influence my decisions?"

If the answer is yes — or even possibly — you should treat it as a conflict of interest and disclose it. You do not need to be certain there is a conflict. If there is a reasonable perception of one, that is enough.

> **Warning:** Do not assume that because you believe you can be fair, others will see it the same way. The perception of a conflict is just as important as the reality.

## Common Situations to Watch For

Be especially alert to conflicts of interest in these situations:

- Procurement or contracting decisions where you have a relationship with a supplier
- Recruitment or performance assessment of people you have personal relationships with
- Regulatory or compliance decisions affecting people or organisations you are connected to
- Policy decisions that could financially benefit you, your family, or associates
- Using government information for personal or private benefit

## Key Takeaways

- Personal interests include both financial and non-financial interests
- Interests of people close to you can also create conflicts
- Use the "reasonable person" test — if someone might perceive a conflict, disclose it
- Common conflict situations involve procurement, recruitment, regulation, and policy
