Module 1: Conflicts of Interest

Lesson 1 Introduction to Conflicts of Interest ▼

A conflict of interest arises when your personal interests — or those of someone close to you — could improperly influence how you carry out your public duties.

Three Types of Conflict

  • Actual — A conflict that currently exists
  • Potential — A conflict that could arise in the future
  • Perceived — A situation where a reasonable person might think your interests could influence your decisions
Important: All three types must be managed. Even a perceived conflict can damage public trust.

Your Obligations

  1. Identify when a conflict exists or may exist
  2. Disclose the conflict to your manager as soon as possible
  3. Manage it appropriately — this may mean stepping back from a decision
Lesson 2 Identifying Conflicts of Interest ▼

Types of Personal Interests

Financial interests: shareholdings, investments, business ownership, secondary employment, financial relationships with organisations your agency deals with.

Non-financial interests: personal relationships, club memberships, previous employment, political affiliations, and strong personal values that may affect impartiality.

Tip: It is not just your own interests that matter — the interests of people close to you (spouse, family, friends) can also create a conflict.

Integrity Town Scenarios

Tama — The Job Application: Tama is on an interview panel; one applicant is his cousin. This is a perceived conflict. He should disclose and step back from assessing his cousin's application.
Lisa — The Supplier: Lisa manages procurement and her brother-in-law runs an approved supplier. Actual conflict. She must disclose and remove herself from related procurement decisions.
Mark — The Side Business: Mark works in policy and started a private consultancy in the same area. Actual conflict. He must disclose and ensure no government information benefits his private work.
Ruth — The Community Board: Ruth sits on a community board; her agency is consulting with that board about funding. Actual and perceived conflict. She must disclose and step back from related work.

The Reasonable Person Test

Ask: "Would a reasonable, impartial person think my personal interests could influence my decisions?" If yes — or possibly — disclose it.

Warning: Do not assume that because you believe you can be fair, others will see it the same way.
Lesson 3 Managing Conflicts of Interest ▼

The Three-Step Process

  1. Identify — Recognise that a conflict exists or may exist
  2. Disclose — Tell your manager as soon as you become aware. Disclose promptly, clearly, and document it.
  3. Manage — Agree on the appropriate management approach
Important: Many agencies have a conflicts of interest register. Ask your manager or HR team how to use it.

Management Options

  • Acknowledge and monitor — Document and continue with heightened transparency (low-risk conflicts)
  • Restrict involvement — Continue in a limited role (e.g. contribute to analysis but not the final decision)
  • Recuse yourself — Step back from the specific decision entirely
  • Transfer responsibility — Permanently reassign that area of work
  • Divest the interest — Sell shares or remove the financial interest
  • Resign from an external role — In extreme cases where the conflict is irreconcilable
Tip: Choose a proportionate response. Work with your manager to find the right balance.

Document Everything

Record: the nature of the conflict, when it was disclosed and to whom, the management approach agreed, and any conditions on your continued involvement.

Module 2: Sensitive Expenditure

Lesson 1 What is Sensitive Expenditure? ▼

Sensitive expenditure is spending that, if it became publicly known, could reasonably embarrass the organisation or government — even if it was technically within the rules.

The Core Test

Would a reasonable member of the public consider this a good use of public money?

Important: The Controller and Auditor-General (OAG) provides guidance on sensitive expenditure for all NZ public sector organisations.

Why It Matters

Poor expenditure decisions can damage trust in the public service, attract negative media coverage, result in OAG findings, and harm careers and reputations.

Common Categories

  • Hospitality — meals, functions, drinks, entertainment
  • Travel — domestic and international, accommodation, class of travel
  • Gifts and koha — given or received in connection with official duties
  • Conferences and events — attendance and sponsorship costs
  • Credit card use — purchases on government credit cards
Tip: If in doubt, don't spend. It is always better to seek approval before incurring an expense than to explain it afterwards.
Lesson 2 Rules and Expectations ▼

The Overarching Principle

All spending must be: necessary, proportionate, transparent, and appropriate.

Authorisation Requirements

  • Hospitality: Requires manager approval above threshold; always document
  • Travel: Must comply with agency policy; international travel needs higher-level approval
  • Gifts: Must comply with agency gifts policy
  • Credit cards: Business purposes only — no personal use permitted
Warning: Retroactive approval is not the same as proper authorisation. You may still be personally liable if you spend without prior approval.

Documentation Requirements

Record: the amount, date, business purpose, names of people involved, and the authorising officer. Keep receipts for all expenses.

Consequences of Breaching the Rules

  • Repayment of misspent funds from your own pocket
  • Formal disciplinary action — up to and including dismissal
  • Adverse findings from the Controller and Auditor-General
  • Reputational damage — personal and organisational
Lesson 3 Categories of Sensitive Expenditure ▼

Hospitality

Must have a genuine business purpose. Legitimate: hosting external guests, working meeting refreshments, team recognition (within limits). Not appropriate: lavish entertainment, no clear purpose, excessive alcohol.

Tip: Hospitality for your own staff is scrutinised more heavily than for external guests.

Travel

Economy class is the default for all public servants. Business class requires specific justification (overnight flight, medical needs). Personal extensions to official travel must be at your own expense.

Warning: Adding personal days to an official trip — even unpaid — can be a problem if it extends accommodation costs.

Gifts and Koha

  • Gifts received in official capacity must be declared; those above threshold (~$50-$100) must be surrendered to the agency
  • Gifts to external parties must be modest; gifts to colleagues using public funds are not appropriate
  • Koha in official engagements with tangata whenua is generally acceptable — follow agency policy

Credit Cards

  • Business purposes only — personal purchases are never permitted, even with intent to repay
  • Keep receipts; reconcile statements regularly

Scenarios

The Overseas Conference: Sarah presents at a 3-day international conference and stays an extra 4 days for a holiday. She must personally fund the extra accommodation and any extended flight costs.
The Farewell Gift: A team wants to buy a $150 retirement gift using departmental funds. Not appropriate — team members should contribute personally.
The Working Lunch: $160 lunch for 8 people at a full-day workshop. Likely appropriate — clear business purpose, modest cost. Documentation of business purpose still required.
Lesson 4 Making Good Decisions ▼

The Five-Question Framework

  1. Genuine business purpose? Can you clearly articulate why this expense is needed?
  2. Proportionate? Is the cost reasonable relative to the benefit?
  3. Within policy? Do you have all required approvals?
  4. Documented? Have you kept receipts and recorded the business purpose?
  5. Newspaper test? Would this embarrass your agency if reported in the media?
Important: Passing all five questions doesn't automatically make expenditure appropriate. Good judgment is also required. When in doubt, seek guidance.

Your Role as an Approver

Approving expenditure means you share responsibility for its appropriateness. Ask questions — don't rubber-stamp requests. Your approval is a personal endorsement of the spending.

Warning: Approving inappropriate expenditure can have the same consequences as incurring it — including repayment obligations and disciplinary action.

Good Habits to Build

  • Always keep receipts — even for small amounts
  • Obtain approval before spending, not after
  • Document the business purpose at the time, not weeks later
  • Report mistakes honestly — concealment always makes things worse
  • When in doubt, ask your manager, finance team, or integrity team first
Quiz Module 2 Quiz — Sensitive Expenditure ▼
Q1: What is the primary test for deciding whether expenditure is appropriate in the public service?
A. Whether it is within the approved budget
B. Whether a reasonable member of the public would consider it a good use of public money ✓
C. Whether your manager has approved it
D. Whether a private sector company would spend the same
The primary test is the reasonable person test. Being within budget or having manager approval doesn't automatically make expenditure appropriate.
Q2: What class of travel should a public servant book by default for an official overseas trip?
A. Business class, to arrive refreshed and productive
B. Economy class, unless there is specific justification for a higher class ✓
C. Premium economy as a standard compromise
D. Whatever class their manager uses
Economy class is the default for all public servants regardless of seniority. Business class requires specific justification such as very long overnight flights or medical needs.
Q3: A public servant accidentally uses their government credit card for personal groceries. What should they do?
A. Say nothing and hope it goes unnoticed
B. Report the error to their manager immediately and arrange to repay ✓
C. Make a future official purchase to offset the personal one
D. Wait until the next card reconciliation
Honest and prompt reporting is the right response. Concealing the error or trying to offset it with future purchases could make the situation much worse.
Q4: Which of the following actions are required when incurring sensitive expenditure? (Select ALL that apply)
A. Obtain prior authorisation where required ✓
B. Keep receipts and documentation ✓
C. Record the business purpose ✓
D. Post about it on social media to demonstrate transparency
E. Ensure it represents value for money ✓
You must obtain prior authorisation, keep receipts, record the business purpose, and ensure value for money. Social media posting is not a transparency requirement.
